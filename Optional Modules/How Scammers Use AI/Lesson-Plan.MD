### **Lesson Plan: How Scammers Use AI**

#### **Audience:** General public (adults, teens, seniors, anyone interested in learning about AI and scams)

#### **Duration:** 60–90 minutes

#### **Format:** Presentation + Interactive Discussion + Hands-on Activity

---

### **Objectives:**

By the end of this lesson, participants will be able to:

1. **Understand how AI is used by scammers** to carry out fraud and deception.
2. **Recognize common signs** of AI-driven scams in emails, social media, and other communication methods.
3. **Learn practical strategies** to protect themselves from AI-based scams.
4. **Take action** by implementing digital safety tips and best practices.

---

### **Materials Needed:**

* **Presentation slides** (PowerPoint or Google Slides)
* **Handouts or digital resources** summarizing key points (optional)
* **Computers/tablets/phones** for hands-on activities (if available)
* **Internet connection** (for live demos or videos)
* **AI detection tools** (optional, for demonstration purposes)

---

### **Lesson Outline:**

#### **1. Introduction (10 minutes)**

* **Greeting & Overview (5 minutes)**:

  * Briefly introduce yourself and explain the topic of the lesson.
  * Ask participants: "Have you ever received a suspicious email or message? What did you do?"
  * Introduce the concept of AI and how it can be used for both positive and negative purposes.
* **Set the Stage (5 minutes)**:

  * Define "scams" and briefly explain what AI is.
  * Present a simple example of an AI-driven scam (e.g., a phishing email, deepfake video) to grab attention.

#### **2. Lesson Content (30–40 minutes)**

**A. How Scammers Use AI (10–15 minutes)**

* **Presentation:** Use slides to cover the following key points:

  1. **Impersonation through AI**: Scammers using deepfake technology to mimic voices and faces.
  2. **Phishing and Email Scams**: How AI generates fake emails and websites that look legitimate.
  3. **Chatbots and Fake Customer Support**: How scammers use AI-powered chatbots to interact with you and gather sensitive data.
  4. **Fake Social Media Accounts and Automated Scams**: AI’s role in creating fake online profiles and spreading malicious links.
* **Discussion:** Ask the group if they’ve ever encountered any of these tactics and share experiences.

**B. How to Spot AI-Driven Scams (10 minutes)**

* **Presentation:** Discuss red flags, such as:

  1. Odd grammar or language.
  2. Unsolicited requests for money or information.
  3. Fake urgency or too-good-to-be-true offers.
  4. Suspicious social media activity.
* **Interactive Q&A:** Have participants brainstorm additional signs of a scam they’ve noticed.

**C. Protecting Yourself from AI Scams (10–15 minutes)**

* **Presentation:**

  1. **Verify contacts**: Always double-check unfamiliar messages.
  2. **Enable two-factor authentication** on accounts.
  3. **Use AI detection tools**: Show examples of tools that can help detect deepfakes and scam websites.
  4. **Stay informed**: Regularly educate yourself on new scam tactics.
* **Discussion:** Encourage participants to share what steps they already take to stay safe online.

---

#### **3. Hands-On Activity (15–20 minutes)**

* **Activity 1: Spot the Scam!**

  * Provide examples of emails, social media posts, or videos (some legitimate, some AI-driven scams).
  * Have participants identify whether each example is a real communication or a scam.
  * Discuss why each example is either safe or suspicious, focusing on signs like poor grammar, fake logos, or unexpected requests.

* **Activity 2: Use an AI Detection Tool (Optional)**

  * If tools are available, walk participants through using an AI detection tool to analyze an example (like a deepfake video or suspicious email).
  * Explain how these tools work and encourage participants to use them on their own.

---

#### **4. Recap & Conclusion (10–15 minutes)**

* **Review Key Takeaways:**

  1. AI is both a powerful tool and a potential threat when used by scammers.
  2. Recognizing the signs of AI-driven scams is key to staying safe.
  3. Simple steps like verifying contacts and enabling security features can help protect you.
* **Closing Discussion:**

  * Ask the group to share one new thing they’ve learned.
  * Encourage participants to talk to friends and family about AI-driven scams to spread awareness.
* **Q&A (5–10 minutes):**

  * Open the floor for any questions or concerns.

---

### **Assessment:**

* **Formative Assessment:** Participation during the hands-on activity and group discussion will show understanding.
* **Summative Assessment:** Optionally, give a short quiz or have participants write down key takeaways as a way to evaluate their grasp of the material.

---

### **Follow-Up:**

* Provide participants with a list of additional resources (websites, videos, or articles) for continued learning.
* Send out an email or handout summarizing key points and providing links to AI detection tools or other helpful resources.

---

### **Optional Extensions:**

* Invite guest speakers from cybersecurity organizations or digital safety experts to discuss the latest threats and ways to protect yourself.
* Hold a follow-up session to dive deeper into specific types of scams (e.g., deepfakes or social media fraud) and explore practical protection techniques.
